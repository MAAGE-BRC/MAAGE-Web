Viral Outbreak Investigation

The Viral Outbreak Investigation workflow guides you through the complete process of genomic surveillance and outbreak analysis—from sequencing data to outbreak interpretation. Whether you are investigating a suspected outbreak, monitoring viral evolution, or comparing isolates to public datasets, MAAGE provides an integrated workspace that supports every stage of the investigation.

Step 1. Upload Your Sequencing Data

Begin by uploading one or more viral samples to your investigation.

MAAGE supports multiple starting points, allowing you to begin with:

  • • Raw sequencing reads (FASTQ)
  • • Assembled viral genome contigs (FASTA)
  • • Previously analyzed viral genomes in your MAAGE workspace

Each uploaded sample becomes part of your investigation and can be analyzed individually or together throughout the workflow.

Supported inputs

  • • Illumina paired-end FASTQ
  • • Oxford Nanopore FASTQ
  • • Assembled viral genomes (FASTA)
  • • Existing MAAGE genomes

Next Step

Run Viral Genome Assembly if starting from raw sequencing reads, or proceed directly to Viral Genome Annotation if assembled genomes are already available.

Step 2. Assemble Viral Genomes

Viral Genome Assembly

If starting from raw sequencing reads, assemble complete viral genomes using MAAGE's viral assembly pipeline.

The assembly workflow reconstructs high-quality viral consensus genomes and generates assembly quality metrics that can be reviewed before downstream analysis.

Outcome

  • • Consensus viral genome
  • • Assembly statistics
  • • Coverage summary
  • • Quality assessment
  • • Genome ready for annotation

Step 3. Annotate Viral Genomes

Viral Genome Annotation

Annotate assembled viral genomes to identify coding sequences, proteins, genomic features, and lineage-specific characteristics.

The annotation workflow generates a comprehensive summary of each viral genome and prepares the genome for downstream comparative analyses.

Outcome

  • • Annotated viral genome
  • • Predicted genes and proteins
  • • Functional annotations
  • • Genome summary
  • • Standardized genome ready for comparison

Step 4. Review Related Public Isolates

Once your viral genome has been assembled and annotated, examine its relationship to publicly available viral genomes.

Compare your genomes against the MAAGE public database to identify closely related isolates, circulating lineages, geographic distribution, and previously reported outbreak sequences.

Use this information to assess:

  • • Closest related public genomes
  • • Geographic distribution
  • • Collection dates
  • • Host and source metadata
  • • Lineage and clade assignments (when available)
  • • Potential epidemiological links

This step often provides the first indication that your samples may be associated with an ongoing outbreak or circulating lineage.

Step 5. Compare Related Isolates with Whole Genome SNP Analysis

If multiple viral genomes appear to be related, perform Whole Genome SNP Analysis to compare them at nucleotide resolution.

Whole Genome SNP Analysis identifies sequence differences across the selected genomes and reports pairwise SNP distances to distinguish closely related isolates.

Use this analysis to:

  • • Confirm outbreak membership
  • • Differentiate highly similar viral isolates
  • • Measure genetic distance
  • • Identify shared and distinguishing mutations

Outputs

  • • SNP matrix
  • • Pairwise SNP distances
  • • Core and complete SNP alignments
  • • Consensus sequences
  • • SNP-based phylogenetic trees

Step 6. Visualize Evolutionary Relationships

Construct phylogenetic trees to summarize the evolutionary relationships among your investigation samples.

The Phylogenetic Analysis service generates publication-quality trees and allows metadata to be mapped directly onto the phylogeny, making it easier to interpret transmission patterns, lineage evolution, geographic spread, and outbreak structure.

Although Whole Genome SNP Analysis also produces phylogenetic trees, this service provides greater flexibility for building customized trees from selected genomes.

Use this step to:

  • • Visualize transmission networks
  • • Explore evolutionary relationships
  • • Compare viral lineages
  • • Generate publication-quality figures
  • • Communicate investigation findings
